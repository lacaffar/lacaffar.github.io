01 / Profile

I make things that
do not fail.

I am a rising third-year mechanical engineering student at NC State (go Pack), graduating in 2028. One idea pulls all of my work together: the future runs on clean, cheap, reliable energy, and I want to build the hardware that delivers it.

That focus on reliability is not abstract for me. In a fusion lab I studied reactor wall stability, the problem of keeping a component intact under the most extreme heat and plasma loads we can produce. Leading Windpack, I learned reliability the hands-on way: design, build, test, find the failure, fix it, repeat. At GE Vernova I now work on wind hardware that has to run in the field for decades.

Reliability engineering at SpaceX is the natural next step. It is the same discipline I already love, pointed at the hardest mechanical problem there is: getting people to the stars and bringing them home. I also spent three years as a Wake County EMS cadet, which is a long way of saying I stay calm when the stakes are real.

02 / Flight Log

The Trajectory

One curiosity, energy, tracked across fusion, wind, and the grid, and pointed at orbit. Select any stage to expand the detail.

Past where it started

Enrolled at NC State to study mechanical engineering with a clear target from day one: energy. Started stacking fundamentals in mechanics, design, and programming, and looked for every chance to put hands on real hardware.

Mechanical Engineering Class of 2028 Wolfpack

In the FPAC (Fusion Plasma Auxiliaries Characterization) Lab, I worked with data from KSTAR, a Korean fusion reactor that briefly recreates the conditions inside a star. My piece was analyzing recent experiments to help develop a system for reactor wall stability, one of the hardest reliability problems standing between us and fusion power.

  • Accessed and analyzed experimental data from a live fusion device.
  • Contributed to work on plasma facing wall stability and component survival.
  • Learned how the power of the stars gets measured, predicted, and contained.
Nuclear Fusion KSTAR Reliability of Components Data Analysis
Present what I am building now

I am President of Windpack, NC State's student club competing in the U.S. Department of Energy Collegiate Wind Competition. We design, fabricate, and test scaled wind turbine systems, and build the sustainability and project development case behind them. It is where I learned that engineering is a team sport, and that a turbine that survives the test stand beats a perfect drawing every time.

  • Lead design, fabrication, and testing of a scaled wind turbine.
  • Own sustainability and project development proposals for the DOE competition.
  • Keep a multidisciplinary student club aligned, funded, and moving.
Wind Energy Team Leadership Design and Fabrication Testing

Right now I work on wind at full scale, as a Wind Engineer Intern at GE Vernova in Greenville, SC, inside one of the companies actually building the energy transition. This is where the student turbine grows up into multi-ton hardware that has to feed real grids and keep running for decades.

  • Hands-on wind engineering work in an on-site role.
  • Learning how energy hardware is designed, validated, and held to a reliability bar.
  • Sharpening the exact instinct SpaceX hires for: assume it will fail, then prove it will not.
Wind Engineering GE Vernova Reliability Engineering at Scale

This is the goal I am working toward. Fusion taught me how components survive extreme loads. Wind taught me to make energy hardware reliable at scale. Reliability engineering at SpaceX is where those threads meet: keeping the hardest machines ever built from failing when failure is not an option.

  • Hope: a reliability engineering role at SpaceX.
