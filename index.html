01 — Mission Profile

Energy is the
whole story.

I'm a rising third-year mechanical engineering student at NC State (go Pack), graduating 2028. I'm here for one reason: I think the future runs on clean, cheap, reliable energy — and I want to build it.

That obsession has taken me to some strange and wonderful places. I've sat in a fusion lab analyzing data from KSTAR, a Korean reactor that recreates the power of the sun. I lead Windpack, NC State's Collegiate Wind Competition team. And I'm now a Wind Engineer Intern at GE Vernova — and yes, I love that the name means verde (green) plus nova (a new star). Energy and the cosmos, in one word.

When I'm not in the lab or the shop, I'm playing volleyball, getting through another history book, or dancing Latin with NC State's Sube team. I also spent three years as a Wake County EMS cadet — which is a long way of saying I stay calm when things get loud.

02 — Flight Log

The Trajectory

One curiosity — energy — chased across fusion, wind, and the grid. Click any stage to expand the telemetry.

Enrolled at NC State (go Pack) to study mechanical engineering, with a clear target from day one: energy. Started stacking the fundamentals — mechanics, design, programming — and looking for every chance to work on real hardware.

Mechanical Engineering Class of 2028 Wolfpack

In the FPAC (Fusion Plasma Auxiliaries Characterization) Lab, I worked with data from KSTAR — a Korean fusion reactor that briefly recreates the conditions inside a star. My piece: analyzing recent experiments to help develop a system for reactor wall stability, one of the hardest problems standing between us and fusion power.

  • Accessed and analyzed experimental data from a live fusion device.
  • Contributed to work on plasma-facing wall stability.
  • Learned how the literal power of the stars gets measured and tamed.
Nuclear Fusion KSTAR Plasma Data Research

I lead Windpack, NC State's team in the U.S. Department of Energy's Collegiate Wind Competition. We design, fabricate, and test scaled wind turbine systems — and build the sustainability and project-development case to back them up. It's where I learned that engineering is a team sport, and that shipping a working turbine beats a perfect drawing every time.

  • Lead design, fabrication, and testing of a scaled wind turbine.
  • Own sustainability and project-development proposals for the DOE competition.
  • Keep a multidisciplinary student team aimed and moving.
Wind Energy Team Leadership CAD & Fabrication Testing

Now I'm working on wind at full scale — as a Wind Engineer Intern at GE Vernova in Greenville, SC, inside one of the companies actually building the energy transition. This is where the student-competition turbine grows up into multi-ton hardware that feeds real grids.

  • Hands-on wind engineering work in an on-site role.
  • Seeing how energy hardware gets designed, validated, and deployed at scale.
  • Living the discipline that aerospace and energy share: get it right, it matters.
Wind Engineering GE Vernova Energy Transition Engineering @ Scale

The next burn. Fusion taught me the power of stars; wind taught me to harness energy at scale. SpaceX is where those threads meet — the hardest mechanical engineering on (and off) the planet. That's the internship I'm aiming for next.

  • Target: summer 2027.
  • Why: controlling enormous amounts of energy is aerospace.
