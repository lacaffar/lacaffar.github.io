Cryogenic Rotary Union

A sub-scale, bench-top superconducting wind turbine cryogenic rotary union, built to move liquid nitrogen across a spinning joint at minus 196 degrees C and prove out the thermal, sealing, and structural engineering that flight cryogenics demand.

The pitch

I am a Mechanical Engineering student at NC State targeting a Summer 2027 Starship engineering internship at SpaceX. My background already runs through extreme engineering: freshman fusion research, a sophomore wind internship at GE Vernova, and an RCA semantic search software project. This build is the capstone that ties it together.

The goal is to design and run a sub-scale, bench-top Superconducting Wind Turbine Cryogenic Rotary Union: a joint that carries liquid nitrogen (LN2, minus 196 degrees C) from a stationary supply into a rotating shaft without leaking, seizing, or boiling off. It is a small machine that forces every hard problem at once: a steep thermal gradient, a dynamic seal at cryogenic temperature, and a structure that has to stay aligned while parts shrink at different rates.

If you can make a rotary union reliable at minus 196 degrees C, you have shown the exact instincts that flight cryogenics at SpaceX require.

The design

First-principles thermal-structural design, with each part chosen to fight a specific failure mode.

316L stainless steel inner fluid shaft

Carries the LN2 through the rotating core. 316L holds toughness at cryogenic temperature and resists the embrittlement that kills lesser steels in the cold.

Vacuum-jacketed and Aerogel insulation

A vacuum jacket plus Aerogel keeps ambient heat out, protects the seal and bearing zones, and limits boil-off so the fluid stays liquid through the joint.

Spring-energized PTFE lip seals

The dynamic sealing problem. Spring energizing keeps the lip loaded as PTFE contracts in the cold, holding a seal on a spinning shaft where a static gasket would fail.

Dry ceramic hybrid bearings, thermally isolated

Hybrid ceramic bearings run dry (no lubricant to freeze) and sit thermally isolated from the cold core, so the rotating assembly stays aligned without conducting the whole heat load into the bearing race.

Why it maps to SpaceX

  • Extreme thermal gradients. Minus 196 degrees C against ambient, across a few centimeters, is the same class of problem as cryogenic propellant hardware.
  • Dynamic sealing. Sealing a moving interface at cryogenic temperature is one of the genuinely hard problems in flight fluid systems.
  • First-principles thermal-structural design. Differential contraction, alignment, and heat paths worked from the physics up, not pulled from a catalog.
  • Reliability mindset. The whole build is an exercise in predicting failure before it happens and designing it out.
